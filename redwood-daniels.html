About

Redwood Daniels is focused on the silence between words. He writes crime stories that feel less like mysteries and more like inevitabilities, often set in the forgotten valleys and deserts of the American West. His work strips away the excess, leaving only the characters, the landscape, and the weight of their choices. He believes that action, not dialogue, tells the truth.

A Silence Between the Wires

Short Fiction

The first mistake was listening. Sheriff Kordell always said that in a town this small, the phone lines carried more truth than the courthouse. But the lines were dead now, thick with the kind of silence that suggests a sudden, surgical removal. It wasn't static; it was absence.

Kordell had been retired for five years, but habits die harder than men out here. He checked his radio first—nothing but the white noise hum. He checked the town's small exchange box outside his window—lights dark. He drove the highway west, tracing the dead wire like a vein that had been cut.

It led him to a turnout twelve miles out, where the high-tension lines crossed the old irrigation ditch. Someone hadn't just cut the wire; they’d used a saw and a cold, clear night. They didn’t want a noise complaint; they wanted quiet. And that made Kordell’s old bones hurt with the kind of certainty he hadn't felt since his daughter disappeared.

He got out, the dry, alkaline dust crunching under his boots. The desert doesn't give up secrets easy, but it always leaves tracks. And standing right there, by the neatly severed line, was a single, clean footprint: too small for a man, too delicate for the dust.

The Bone Orchard Drift - Chapter 1

Excerpt from a work in progress

The drift always comes back. In Port Sulfur, the desert doesn't just surround you; it moves into your lungs and your foundation. You spend your life sweeping it away, knowing that by morning, the pale, fine dust will have crossed your threshold again, leaving a fresh, clean layer over everything you thought you'd finished.

Sheriff Eli Kincaid was done sweeping. He stood on the edge of his property, where the fence met the old bone orchard—a stretch of scrubland where nothing but bleached wood and dry sage grew. The morning sun was a brutal, honest thing, but it hadn't stopped the killer from leaving their signature right there in the dirt.

The body wasn't hidden. It was laid out, almost respectful, facing the rising sun. Kincaid knew the face. Everyone knew the face. It belonged to the kind of man who owed everyone a debt he could never pay back. But the way he was killed—clean, quiet—suggested an accountant, not a thug. Someone who was balancing the books.

Kincaid crouched down, the heat already rising off the cracked earth. He hadn't been wearing his badge for six months. He was just a man with a piece of property that bordered the town's shame, and now, its new history. He touched the dust, feeling the weight of the silence. It told him that in Port Sulfur, being retired meant nothing. Some debts just transfer ownership.

Redwood Daniels

Redwood Daniels grew up in the valleys and deserts of California, places where industry and nature fight a losing war. That landscape, heavy with history and silence, is the constant backdrop for his stories. He is less concerned with *who* committed the crime than with the weight that crime imposes on the people left behind.

He writes slowly, deliberately, and always with the intent to cut every sentence to its most necessary form. His work seeks to strip away the pretense of civilization, exploring what happens when the law stops paying attention and the local code takes over. He currently lives near the coast, where the highway ends and the real shadows begin.

The Code

  • **No saints. No monsters.**
  • **Action tells the truth.**
  • **Silence matters.**
  • **The consequence is the story.**
  • **The land remembers.**
